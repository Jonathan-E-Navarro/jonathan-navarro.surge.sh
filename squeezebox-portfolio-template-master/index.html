My first real challenge

Oh othello, how you put me down. But rise again I did!

If I'm going to be honest. I failed this project the first time around. But that isnt to say that my work was without merit. I learned possibly the most important thing that most software engineers or CS students tend to dread like boogeyman himself. Failure and its inevitability.

I took the class that this project was featured in, in the winter quarter of my freshman year. Mind you, I was an eager freshman with absolutely zero programming expereince prior to arriving at UC Irvine. All I knew was that I loved technology and was dead set on pursuing a career that allowed me to aid in its creation and innovation.

This project and class as a whole however, was wakeup call to what software engineering would hold in store for me. Up until this point, my programming was making simple functions and a console based connnect four game. This was my first GUI, the first piece of code that I could click, see color, and interact with the way I interact with any other program on my computer.

The process that was needed to make this a reality was a little hard for me to grasp at first, and the impending time crunch at hand gave my first dose of working on something I didnt necessarily know if I could finish, espcially on time. Alas the due date came and what was turned in, was an incomplete project that made me feel so inadequate.

Although I was not the only one to have struggled with the class, the overarching atmosphere didnt seem to make me feel better. Among my classmates there was talks of switching majors, doubt about their inability to pursue such a daunting career.

As I write this, I close towards my final year as a computer science undergraduate. Meaning, (thank goodness), that I did not waiver. I realized that accepting failure was a part of the game, and that my professors instance that many of us give up and switch out was an attempt to thin the herd so to speak.

Well I didn't buy it, I acknowledged the fact that some people are naturally good at programming and computer science, but that didnt meant that I couldn't be as well. I knew that would have to work twice as long or hard for the same result that somebody else could have hashed out in no time. But one thing that would give me the advantage, was the fact that I would have experience dealing with pressure and stress.

I would have to retake this class as well as another. The summer break of my Junior year into my Senior year would end up being my first and only realy break of college as a I spent my summers either retaking or taking other classes in advance, in order to ensure a timely graduation.

Looking back this project might not be the most complex, but it certainly had a big impact on my journey as an aspiring software engineer.

Fear the wumpus!

My first foray into the world of artificial intelligence

This assignment was felt rather daunting at first, but ended up being one of the more enjoyable projects that I've worked on.

The name of the game is Wumpus, the objective of this game is have our hero traverse a map of rooms separated by doors so that he/she can retrieve a chest full of treasure. The map however is not devoid of obstacles and dangers. Somewhere in the map lies the monster known as the wumpus, as well as a multitude of bottomless pits, and if our hero were to have the misfortune of stepping into one of these rooms, our hero perishes. The one tool our hero has to aid them in their journey is their senses, if the wumpus is in any of the adjacent rooms, the hero will percieve a stench and a breeze in the case of nearby pits.

The way I went about creating survival heuristics was probably the most enjoyable part of the project. I walked through a visual representation of the wumpus map, and put my theoretical character through a number of different scenarios in order to see if there was any discernable pattern that I could percieve. What I ended up noticing was that having the character move around the map in a snake like fashion while taking note of various percepts in the environment gave the character a siginifcantly high chance of finding the gold.

For example, if the hero perceives a stench in the beginning tile, it can only go one of two ways, each with a 50% chance of meeting their doom. In the case where the hero survives this first obstacle, the hero can then commence its snake routine. What this does is that while snaking around map and taking note of perceived smells, the hero can then make educated guesses about where these dangers actually are and avoid the suspected tiles. Once the hero has found the gold, or has run out of safe tiles to explore (in the case that the gold has been spawned with the wumpus or inside of a pit), then the hero can merely backtrack on its noted safe tiles and exit the map safely.

Embedded Systems

One of my absolute favorite classes at UCI

As part of my embedded systems class at UCI, we were tasked with building multiple projects over the course of 10 weeks, of which I will telling you all about my favorite, the music maker. The body of this project is comprised of a 9v power battery for power, the atmel atmega32 chip, an LCD, a keypad for input and a speaker for output.

One of my goals of this project was to speed up song creation so that I could play around and load different songs into it faster. To do this, instead of creating songs as a collection of frequencies and durations, I mapped two octaves worth of frequencies to their corresponding alphabetical representations and durations to their corresponding musical terms.
